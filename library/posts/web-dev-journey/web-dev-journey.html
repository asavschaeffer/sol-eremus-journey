Introduction

My A Dream and B Dream

  When I realized the best way to work in game development is to show that you can make games, I was very far from the world of programming that I was once immersed in as a student. It was nearly midnight in San Francisco and I was helping to close down the restaurant I worked at. If the chef knew I was still there, he would be furious because the management was always cracking down on overtime. But, on the inside, I knew he was proud. He would see that I had been working since 6:00AM when he checked over the books anyway. The task was simple but terrifying: get the trash bins out of the basement. The elevator, like the ovens, drainage, water-heater, lights, roof, and everything else of dire importance in the restaurant would at times betray us. The chapparitos opened up the metal hatch and waited in the alley. The rest of the dish boys and the güero lifted up the garbage bin, pushing it over their heads, and the boys in the alley would pull it through. It would slip, arms would give out, it was not safe. I genuinely would not be surprised if I came into work one morning to find a smashed corpse under a garbage bin.

  But nothing worse than trash juice fell on us that day and I headed back to my girlfriend’s house in the ‘loin. She was the pastry chef at the restaurant and she had a lil hijo who loved playing Free Fire. I thought to myself, what a piece of shit rip-off game… and I, for the first time in a long time, I went through the cycle of thoughts that only a person who regrets ignoring their dream feels. At that time, I was happy working at the restaurant, it had a great potential for me. I got the job by randomly running into the owner on the street during winter break of my senior year of college and talking to him about opening a picnic restaurant in a garden. He said it was a thing in China and it had potential in the U.S. too. I had really fucked everything up in college because I liked learning but not working, so I had no job prospects and went all in on being a chef and opening a garden restaurant, “The Slow Coat” somewhere along highway 1. If Sam’s Chowder Bar could be packed every damn time I drove by, so too could my grand ideas bear fruit. I worked at his restaurant for a year, starting at salad, moving to pastry, sides, fry, sarten, and finally my last 3 months private events. They were training me to be the chef, and I really sacrificed everything during that year to the restaurant. I made great friends, dated an inspiring, strong, and beautiful woman, learned what hard work truly is, experienced pressure, witnessed all sorts of Bourdain-esque drama, and build a confidence for myself that I will never lose. Also, I learned how to make a lot of good food.

  The truth is I cannot deny my true dream is to be a hero. I’ve wanted magic powers since I was in the 3rd grade and I realized life was so mundane. In the time between then and now I have gained an appreciation for boring life, and experienced magic through Surfing, learning, and sadhana. Even so, constantly I am filled with inspiration, ideas generated from the strangest things, to put within my other world. I couldn’t sublimate it any longer. It feels like destiny. I can’t stop thinking about it. I want to build another world, that is more than a just video game, where people can experience magic and freedom. And I know if I don’t do it, and somebody else releases a game that captures the world’s attention, I will be a monster of jealousy and regret for the rest of my life. In the end, I dumped her brutally and betrayed the restaurant and gave up the B dream: “The Slow Coast: picnic with 3 stars”

Why Web Development?

I went on a snowboarding trip with my dad to Japan and ended up staying for 2 months there with a Japanese girl. I hunted hard for a job doing anything in the Tokyo game industry. I applied for so many jobs that recruiters were kindly asking me to stop bothering them with worthless applications. I spent 20 hours on a 3D modeling task that I actually think was a masterpiece only to be denied a first interview. I spent thousands on a courses from Udemy, Zenva Academy, Stacksocial, ELVTR, and Codecademy.

I failed and went to live with my dad in San Diego because no job no gaijin. I had a nice summer with him, working as a valet with him at the Marriott Marquis. I found a volunteer game design job working for P1, and I got initiated into Shambhavi by Sadhguru at the LA convention center. We went on a surf trip to a little island chain tucked between Nias and the Ments, and I returned to Japan to see my girl and try and work for From Software. I was absorbed again, becoming another drone in the endless depth and breadth of Tokyo. I learned a lot from P1, but it became hard to attend the meetings with the time difference and I’ve ended up putting my volunteer tasks there on the back burner in favor of my grand plan. The best thing I learned from them was references, then portfolio, then experience, skills, certification, or whatever. Truly, the only thing that matters are references. How do we get references? We meet people and show them value. Portfolio based jobs show value via a sample, such as an artist on Artstation, or a musician on youtube. Game Developers have github, and itch.io. Everybody has a personal website. I went to the 2023 Tokyo Game show with no website, just some fraudulent business cards and a dream. I convinced many people I was a researcher and that I could help the design of their game using psychology and neuroscience. Talking to them gave me a lot of energy and drive at the time, and I even was given a pass to the afterparty and the following days by one game studio that I got along with. I organized all the business cards, pamphlets, and scraps into an annotated and color-coded mailing list. I still have yet to message any of them. My website just isn’t ready!

I saw a lot of websites for my colleagues at P1 and even the best game designers had barebones websites. Now, I don’t mean their actual website content. At that time, every last one of them had more game development experience than me. I only mean that if I make a website, from scratch, I can stand out from the rest of the weebly and wix cookie cutter portfolio sites. I will watch the video of the game demo, but it would be better to play it for myself. Maybe it’s just me, but I don’t download random games from portfolio sites. It’s just a hassle to set them up. I realized then that if I have a solid understanding of web development, I can 1) make an excellent website for myself 2) make some side income 3) Make web games.

Using the website as a game is the best way to show value that I can think of. Any potential connection can look at your site, on their phone, and see if you are up to snuff. Thankfully at least one of my desperate course purchases was useful, and I found a full certification program for “Front End Engineer” on Codecademy. Well, if I have little game dev experience, and no web game, I have to fill my website with something! That’s why I’m writing this blog post!

My Plan to Fill the Website

My plan for the website content is a landing page with impressive artwork and some mission statements. Each has a variety of links to different blog posts describing further the mission statement sub-ideas. At the bottom of the page is a secret link to the web game in the center of a whirlpool. The blog features tons of content, basically whatever I want and lots of stuff aimed toward search engine optimization. For the resume I aim to organize it like a history. At the top I can have a header and a brief description, something like I approach the life as a warrior-priest. Below that are projects and skills. The projects are expandable, and are linked to the skills used. Each skill is a combination of applications and websites, which will be displayed alongside the amount of time I have spent using said application or website. Below that is volunteer experience and education, certifications. The overall organization and structure of this page is still under progress. Would it be better to show certifications with the skills for example or the education section?

Informed Design Database

I also was inspired when at the 2023 Tokyo Game Convention to make a website for some of the Informed Design work I did for Tumblegotchi and Skyjellies. Basically this is a database for anything related to the design of interactive media. I imagine the layout is simple header with the logo, the nav contains [home] [forum] [newsletter] [consulting] [team]. The homepage has a search for research, with some categories like sense (sight, hearing, smell, touch), media type (board game, mobile, console, computer, vr, advertising, café), content type (research, documentation, journalism, post-mortems, etc.), publishing date & more. Document Reader AI

The entries of the database are scraped from the internet far and wide. An AI is trained to identify various themes from a research journal article or game design documentation.

Search

The entries into the database are indexed and searched via this priority:

  1. words in the search entry matching words in the document title
  2. words in the search entry transform to themes matching words in the document title
  3. words in the search entry matching tags in the website
  4. words in the search entry transform to themes matching tags in the website
  5. words in the search entry matching keywords identified in the document
  6. words in the search entry transform to themes matching keywords identified in the document
  7. words in the search entry matching any words in the document
  8. words in the search entry transform to themes matching any themes in the document

This cascades down, such that if the condition is met the rest are delayed and the results are displayed while the rest keeps searching. For example, search "eye pain", supposing the database has three documents titled “Ocular Strain in Humans by Dr. Froid”, “Health and Nature vol. 13”, and “Neurobiological Effects of Caffeine” on the muscular system". Suppose the keywords for “Health and Nature vol. 13” are: soil, smell, relaxation, trees” but the content contains phrase: “Exposure to the natural light of the sun increases alertness and happiness”. Support the content for “Neurobiological Effects of Caffeine” doesn’t contain the word eye or pain, but the keywords are: focus, anxiety, fear, stress.

The search entry eye pain misses (1) for all 3 documents because none contain the exact word “eye”, “pain”, or “eye pain”. It catches “Ocular Strain in Humans by Dr. Froid” at (2), and immediately begins loading that as the priority. All documents are analyzed for theme when they are added into the database. In the background, it is indexing and skips (3) & (4) for “Health and Nature vol. 13” and “Neurobiological Effects of Caffeine” because no tags were included in the search. There are no direct matches for keywords in the document, so nothing at (5) It catches “Neurobiological Effects of Caffeine” at (6), because the tags of anxiety, fear, and stress link to the theme of negativity that pain is included in. It adds “Neurobiological Effects of Caffeine” to the loading queue behind “Ocular Strain in Humans by Dr. Froid”, and continues indexing. It skips (7) for “Health and Nature vol. 13 because neither “eye”, “pain” nor “eye pain” were mentioned in the document text. “eye pain” connects to the theme of visual strain, and the sentence “Exposure to the natural light of the sun” connects to the theme as well. At (8), therefore, “Health and Nature vol. 13” is added to the loading queue and eventually rendered.
